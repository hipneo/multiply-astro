The founder pulled up his org chart on the call and pointed at a box that said Head of Marketing. He had hired her eight months earlier at $120,000 a year. She was good. Genuinely good. The briefs were sharp, the campaigns shipped on time, the content calendar was full, the social channels were alive for the first time in the company's history. On paper, the hire had worked.

And yet he was frustrated in a way he could not quite name. So I asked him a simple question. Who decides what you market, and to whom, and why?

He went quiet. Because the honest answer was: he did. He was still the one choosing the positioning. He was still the one deciding which channels got budget. He was still the one in the shower on Sunday night working out what the company's actual growth story was. His Head of Marketing was executing beautifully. She just was not deciding anything. He had hired an executor when what the company needed was a leader, and he had spent eight months and six figures finding that out the slow way.

## Fractional CMO vs Head of Marketing: the distinction that costs founders the most

**A fractional CMO owns the marketing strategy, sets the direction, and is accountable for growth outcomes. A Head of Marketing executes within a strategy that already exists. If your company doesn't have a clear, working marketing strategy yet, hiring a Head of Marketing before a fractional CMO is the most expensive way to find that out.**

That is the whole thing in three sentences. The rest of this post is about why the difference is so easy to miss, and how to know which one your company actually needs right now. Because the choice between a **fractional cmo or head of marketing** is not a matter of budget or seniority preference. It is a matter of which problem you are actually solving.

## What a Head of Marketing actually is, and isn't

A Head of Marketing is an executor. That is not an insult. Execution is where marketing lives or dies, and a good one is worth every dollar. But be clear about what you are buying.

A Head of Marketing runs campaigns, manages channels, and produces content. They take a direction and turn it into shipped work. They are typically five to eight years into their career, strong in one or two disciplines, maybe performance and lifecycle, maybe content and brand. They are excellent at making a plan real.

What they are not is a strategist by default. They need strategic direction to be effective, because their skill is turning a decision into output, not making the decision in the first place. They report to someone who owns the strategy: a CMO, a founder, or a fractional CMO. Fully loaded, a Head of Marketing costs $90,000 to $140,000 a year.

Give a strong Head of Marketing a clear strategy and they will outperform your expectations. Give them a blank page and ask them to figure out the whole thing, and you will watch a talented person quietly drown.

## What a fractional CMO actually is, and isn't

A [fractional CMO](/blog/what-does-a-fractional-cmo-do) is the opposite hire in almost every dimension.

They own the strategy, they do not just execute it. They are typically 15 or more years in, and they have built and run full marketing functions before, more than once, usually across companies that look a lot like yours. That pattern recognition is the product. They can operate without strategic direction because they are the ones who create it. Hand them a blank page and that is exactly where they do their best work.

They work part-time, across a small number of companies, which is what makes the economics work. A fractional CMO costs $5,000 to $10,000 a month, which is a $60,000 to $120,000 a year equivalent for two to three days a week. For that, you get someone who has already made the mistakes you are about to make and knows which turns to skip. If you are weighing this against a senior full-time hire, I have written a full breakdown of [fractional CMO vs full-time CMO](/blog/fractional-cmo-vs-full-time-cmo) that covers the cost and flexibility math, and a tier-by-tier [fractional CMO cost comparison](/blog/fractional-cmo-rates) if you want to see what each band buys.

Notice what just happened. The Head of Marketing and the fractional CMO are not two points on the same ladder, junior and senior versions of one job. They are two different jobs. One designs the system. The other runs it.

## The core distinction: strategy versus execution

Here is the belief that gets founders into trouble. It sounds so reasonable that almost nobody questions it: a senior marketing hire will figure out the strategy.

They won't. Not because they are not smart, but because strategy and execution are different skills, different mindsets, and built from different experience. A great executor is not automatically a strategist. A great strategist is not always a great executor. Asking one to be the other is like asking your best chef to design the restaurant. Related, sure. The same job, no.

Strategy is positioning, ICP, channel mix, messaging, and the growth model. It is the set of decisions about what game you are playing and why you can win it. Execution is campaigns, content, ads, email, social, and SEO. It is everything that happens after those decisions are made.

You need the strategy before the execution, or the execution produces the wrong things with great efficiency. A Head of Marketing executing the wrong strategy is not a slow failure. It is a fast one, dressed up as productivity. Dashboards climbing, work shipping, everyone busy, and none of it compounding, because the whole system is pointed at the wrong target. Efficient failure is still failure. It just takes longer to notice.

## The decision framework: five questions

When founders ask me about **when to hire head of marketing vs cmo**, I do not give them a philosophy. I give them five questions. Answer honestly.

**Q1: Do you have a clear, validated marketing strategy?** If no, you need a fractional CMO first. You cannot hire someone to execute a strategy that does not exist.

**Q2: Do you know exactly which channels are working, and why?** If you are guessing, or if the honest answer is "we think LinkedIn, maybe," you need a fractional CMO first.

**Q3: Is your CAC and LTV understood and healthy?** If you cannot state both numbers with confidence, you need strategy before you need more execution. Fractional CMO first.

**Q4: Do you have someone senior enough to direct a Head of Marketing?** A Head of Marketing needs a strategic boss. If that boss does not exist, or is you doing it at 11pm, you need a fractional CMO first.

**Q5: Is your main constraint ideas and strategy, or bandwidth and execution?** This is the deciding question. If you know exactly what to do and simply lack the hands to do it, that is a bandwidth problem, and a Head of Marketing is your hire. If you are not sure what to do in the first place, that is a strategy problem, and no amount of hands solves it.

Four of those five point the same way for a reason. Most companies asking this question do not yet have the strategy layer figured out. They just feel the pain as "we need more marketing" and reach for the most tangible fix.

## When a Head of Marketing is the right hire

Sometimes it genuinely is the right hire, and I want to be fair about that.

Hire a Head of Marketing when you have a proven, working marketing system already. When your constraint is more hands to execute it, not more thinking to design it. When you have a CMO or fractional CMO already providing strategic direction, so the new hire has someone to report into and a strategy to run inside. And usually when you are at $5M or more in ARR with established channels that you already know work.

If that is you, a Head of Marketing is a fantastic hire and a fractional CMO would be overkill. You do not need a new strategy. You need someone to run the one you have, harder and better.

## When a fractional CMO is the right hire

Hire a [fractional CMO for SaaS](/blog/fractional-cmo-for-saas) when you do not have a clear marketing strategy yet. When your founder is still the de facto CMO, making every real call. When you have tried marketing hires before and they did not move the needle, which is almost always a sign they were executing without a strategy underneath them. And usually when you are at $1M to $5M in ARR, still figuring out which channels to double down on.

This is the stage where the wrong hire is most expensive, because a Head of Marketing here inherits a blank page they were never equipped to fill. You do not have a bandwidth problem. You have a direction problem. Hands do not fix direction.

## The hybrid path that works best

Here is the sequence I have watched work again and again, and it resolves the whole dilemma.

Hire the fractional CMO first. Let them build the strategy and prove the channels, so you actually know what works and why. Once the system runs, once there is a real strategy and a set of channels producing predictable results, hire a Head of Marketing to run it. The fractional CMO then transitions to advisory as the Head of Marketing gets up to speed, handing off a machine instead of a mystery.

This is not a compromise. It is how you hire a Head of Marketing who can actually succeed. You are no longer asking a five-to-eight-year executor to invent a strategy from nothing. You are handing them a proven system and clear KPIs, which is exactly the job they are built for. The fractional CMO makes the eventual Head of Marketing hire work, instead of watching it fail the slow, expensive way that founder on the call discovered. If you decide to go this route, here is [how to hire a fractional CMO](/blog/how-to-hire-a-fractional-cmo) without the usual mistakes.

Strategy first, then execution. Every time. The founder with the great Head of Marketing did not make a bad hire. He made a good hire in the wrong order.

## FAQ

**What is the difference between a fractional CMO and a Head of Marketing?**

A fractional CMO owns the marketing strategy, sets the direction, and is accountable for growth outcomes. A Head of Marketing executes within a strategy that already exists. The fractional CMO decides what to do and why. The Head of Marketing turns those decisions into shipped work. They are two different jobs, not junior and senior versions of the same one.

**Should I hire a fractional CMO or a marketing manager?**

If you do not yet have a clear, validated strategy, hire the fractional CMO first. A marketing manager or Head of Marketing needs a strategy to execute against, and hiring one before that strategy exists means paying a talented person to guess. Once the strategy is proven, a marketing manager becomes the right hire to run it day to day.

**When should a startup hire a Head of Marketing?**

When you have a proven marketing system, established channels you know work, and someone senior providing strategic direction. Usually that is around $5M or more in ARR. Before that, most startups need strategy more than they need execution bandwidth, which points to a fractional CMO first. The same logic applies to the [fractional CMO vs marketing director](/blog/fractional-cmo-vs-full-time-cmo) question: title matters less than whether the strategy already exists.

**Can a fractional CMO work with a Head of Marketing?**

Yes, and it is often the ideal setup. The fractional CMO owns strategy and direction while the Head of Marketing owns execution. The fractional CMO sets the plan, the Head of Marketing runs it, and the two together cover both halves of the job at a fraction of the cost of a full-time CMO plus a separate strategist.
